"Throughout the 20th century, various plastics were developed and widely used in different decades. Here's a general overview of some of the main plastics used in each decade from the 1900s to the 1990s:

1900s: The first synthetic plastic, Bakelite, was invented in 1907 by Leo Baekeland. It was widely used for electrical insulators, jewelry, and household items.

1910s-1920s: Celluloid and cellulose acetate were popular plastics during this time, used for items such as film, toys, and consumer goods.

1930s-1940s: Polyvinyl chloride (PVC) and polystyrene were introduced and gained popularity for use in various applications, including pipes, packaging, and consumer products.

1950s: Polyethylene (PE) and polypropylene (PP) became widely used for packaging, containers, and household items due to their flexibility, lightweight nature, and low cost.

1960s: Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS) and polycarbonate (PC) were introduced, offering improved strength and durability for use in electronic devices, automotive parts, and consumer goods.

1970s: Thermoplastic elastomers (TPE) and polyurethane (PU) gained popularity for their flexibility and durability, used in applications such as footwear, automotive parts, and coatings.

1980s: Engineering plastics like polyamide (PA, also known as nylon) and polybutylene terephthalate (PBT) were increasingly used in automotive, electrical, and electronic applications due to their high strength and heat resistance.

1990s: Biodegradable plastics, such as polylactic acid (PLA), were developed and gained attention for their environmental benefits. Traditional plastics like PE, PP, PVC, and PET continued to be widely used in various industries." - Gab ai

"Capacitor and DC (Direct Current): A capacitor opposes changes in voltage. When a DC voltage is applied across a capacitor, it will initially act as a short circuit, allowing current to flow. As the capacitor charges up, the current flow will decrease, and eventually, the capacitor will block the DC current once it is fully charged. In this sense, a capacitor can be said to "block DC" in steady-state conditions.

Inductor and AC (Alternating Current): An inductor opposes changes in current. When an AC voltage is applied across an inductor, the inductor will impede the flow of current, as the changing current induces a magnetic field that generates a voltage opposing the change in current. The higher the frequency of the AC voltage, the greater the inductor's opposition to current flow (inductive reactance). In this sense, an inductor can be said to "block AC," especially at higher frequencies." -gabAi
